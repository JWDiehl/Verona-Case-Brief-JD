01 — Situation

A profitable mid-cap industrial that has run out of headroom.

GE Verona manufactures precision rotating-equipment assemblies (industrial pumps and compressor units) for energy, water, and process-industry customers. Demand is strong and the order book is growing, but the company is increasingly quoting lead times it cannot reliably hit.

What's going well

Order intake is up roughly 9% year-on-year and the sales pipeline is the strongest in a decade. Gross margins are healthy at ~34%. Quality, measured by field-return rate, sits at 1.8% — a number the company markets on.

What's hurting

On-time delivery has slipped from 94% to 81% over six quarters. The plants are quoting 14+ week lead times against a market norm of 9–10. Two large framework customers have put renewals "under review," citing reliability.

The CEO's mandate is blunt: lift effective annual throughput by 30% within 30 months — enough to recover delivery performance and absorb the growing book — without compromising the 1.8% quality number and without letting unit cost creep up. "Effective" throughput is defined as units shipped on-time-in-full, not units produced.

02 — The decision on the table

The board is split two ways — and that split is the reason Slide3 was engaged.

Both camps agree on the 30% target. They disagree on where the throughput will come from. Neither side has yet established where the true constraint actually sits.

Camp A · "Build capacity"

Pour capital into a new factory

  • Believes the plants are physically maxed out and no amount of process work creates 30% more capacity.
  • Proposes a fourth plant (greenfield or major expansion of Plant 2).
  • Argues a new line lets the company hold quality by not overloading existing equipment.
  • Concern from the other camp: high capital cost, long build time, risk of building capacity the constraint isn't in.
Camp B · "Fix what we have"

Squeeze the plants & fix order management

  • Believes throughput is lost to process friction, not physical limits — long changeovers, WIP pileups, and a slow front office.
  • Points to the order-management process as a hidden bottleneck before parts are even cut.
  • Argues capital should go to debottlenecking and systems, not concrete.
  • Concern from the other camp: incremental gains may not reach 30%; "fixing process" is harder to guarantee on a board timeline.
Slide3's brief: diagnose the true constraint, then recommend an operational path to +30% effective throughput within 30 months and the capital envelope. The deliverable is a defensible diagnosis — not a vote for whichever camp is louder.
03 — The system at a glance

From a customer order to a shipped unit, the work crosses two worlds.

Throughput is gated by the whole chain — the front-office order process and the physical shop floor. A unit cannot ship faster than the slowest stage it passes through. The team's first job is to find that stage.

Watch-out for the team: elapsed time per stage is not the same as where capacity is constrained. A stage can be slow because it's genuinely capacity-limited, or because work piles up in queue ahead of it. Distinguishing the two is the core of the diagnosis.
04 — The three plants

Three plants, three different stories.

The plants are not interchangeable. They run different product mixes, different equipment ages, and very different utilization profiles. Aggregate numbers hide this — the team should resist averaging.

Plant 1 — Verona

The volume engine. Runs near nameplate but OEE at the CNC cell suggests run-time losses. Low cost, good quality. Little obvious slack — unless OEE is the lever.

Plant 2 — Brescia

Reportedly the most "maxed out," but high downtime, long changeovers, and the worst quality and unit cost. Is it truly full, or full of friction?

Plant 3 — Padua

Sits at 58% utilization with a big labor vacancy. Idle capacity on paper — but is it the right capacity for the demand mix, and can it be staffed?

Note on OEE: Overall Equipment Effectiveness = Availability × Performance × Quality. A bottleneck cell at 64% OEE has meaningful theoretical recovery before any new equipment is bought — but recovering it is an operational program, not a guarantee. The team should decompose OEE before treating it as free throughput.
05 — The order-management process

Half the lead time is consumed before a single part is cut.

Camp B's claim rests here. Of the ~14.2-week average lead time, roughly 7.3 weeks elapse in the front office (order entry, engineering, scheduling) before material is even released. The team should test whether this is a capacity constraint, a policy constraint, or both.

Table 5.1 — Front-office stage analysis: elapsed vs. touch time (fictional data).

Where the front office friction shows up

  • ~30% of orders need at least one round of clarification with the customer or sales before they can be configured.
  • Engineering is a shared pool of 11 engineers serving all three plants with no triage between standard and custom work.
  • Production scheduling runs on a weekly batch release — an order that misses the Friday cutoff waits.
  • There is no single owner of an order end-to-end; it changes hands 6+ times.

What the data does not yet tell you

  • Whether front-office delay actually limits shipped units, or just lengthens lead time on units that ship anyway.
  • How much of the 30% clarification rate is standard vs. custom orders.
  • Whether faster order release would simply move the queue downstream onto the plant bottleneck.
  • The true demand the system would see if lead times were competitive (see Section 7).
Classic trap: speeding up a non-constraint feels productive but adds no throughput — it just grows WIP in front of the real constraint. Before recommending order-process fixes, establish whether the front office is the constraint or merely a delay.

07 — Demand & mix

Throughput only matters against the demand it has to serve.

A 30% throughput lift is only valuable if the demand is real and the mix matches the capacity. The team should sanity-check the target against the order book before optimizing the factory.

Demand signal

  • Order intake up ~9% YoY; backlog has grown from 6 to 11 weeks of shipments.
  • Lost-order analysis suggests ~$40M of annual revenue declined or lost to lead time in the last year.
  • Two framework customers (~18% of revenue combined) tie renewal to on-time delivery recovery.

Mix considerations

  • ~62% of volume is standard product; ~38% is custom or semi-custom (which loads engineering hardest).
  • Custom orders carry higher margin but disproportionate front-office and changeover cost.
  • Plant 3's idle capacity is configured mostly for custom/mixed work — not the standard volume that's most backed up.

08 — Open questions for the team

The questions the analysis needs to answer.

These are unanswered on purpose. They are the spine of the diagnostic — not a checklist of conclusions.

Where is the true system constraint — front office, a specific plant cell, labor, or material? Is there one constraint or a moving one?
How much of the gap is recoverable throughput (OEE, changeover, OTIF) vs. genuinely new capacity that must be built or staffed?
Does the +30% effective target require +30% produced units, or can on-time recovery close part of it with existing output?
If the front office is sped up, does throughput rise — or does WIP just relocate to a downstream plant bottleneck?
Is Plant 2 truly capacity-maxed, or is its 95% utilization masking downtime, changeover, and quality losses?
Can Plant 3's idle capacity be staffed given a 22% vacancy rate, and does its configuration match the backed-up demand mix?
Which combination of levers fits inside $48M and 30 months while holding quality ≤1.8% and unit cost ≤100?
What's the sequencing — which levers must come first because later ones depend on them, and which buy time?
What are the leading KRIs / early signals that would tell the board at month 6 whether the chosen path is working?

09 — Your task

What to produce — and what this file deliberately leaves to you.

This scenario file is the starting dataset, not the answer. Use it to run your own constraint diagnosis and build a defensible path.

Suggested deliverables

  • A diagnosis of the true constraint, with the evidence that rules out the alternatives.
  • A throughput bridge: today → +30% effective, broken into recovery vs. new capacity.
  • A sequenced 30-month operational path that fits the $48M envelope.
  • The board recommendation: build, fix, or a specific blend — and why.
  • Risks, dependencies, and month-6 leading indicators.

Useful frameworks to reach for

  • Theory of Constraints — identify, exploit, subordinate, elevate.
  • Value-stream mapping — elapsed vs. value-added time across the chain.
  • OEE decomposition — availability × performance × quality.
  • Little's Law — WIP, throughput, and lead-time relationships.
  • Capital option sizing against the envelope and timeline.
Ground rule: let the data pick the constraint before you pick a camp. The board split (build vs. fix) is a hypothesis to test, not a menu to choose from. A strong answer might be neither, or both in sequence.

The bar: board-level quality

The deliverable will be read by senior leaders who own the decision but not the detail. It is judged on whether a smart non-specialist can follow the logic and act on it — not on how sophisticated the analysis looks.

  • Lead with the answer. Every page opens with the takeaway; the supporting analysis sits underneath, not in front.
  • Define before you use. Spell out OEE, OTIF, constraint, WIP the first time — assume the room has not seen them.
  • Always answer "so what." Tie each finding to a dollar, a unit, a week, or a decision the board can make.
  • One idea per page. If a leader needs prior context to understand a slide, it is not board-ready yet.
  • Recommendation up front. State what you'd do and why in plain language before the evidence trail.

01 — Recommendation

What we'd do, and why — before the evidence trail.

The board was split on where to spend $48M: a new plant, or fixing what exists. Both camps agreed on the target and disagreed on the mechanism. The diagnosis below shows the data supports neither pure camp.

Camp A said

Build a new plant

Plants are physically maxed out; no process work creates 30% more capacity. Fund a 4th plant or a major Plant 2 expansion.

Camp B said

Fix what we have

Throughput is lost to process friction and a slow front office, not physical limits. Capital should go to debottlenecking, not concrete.

What the data shows

Fix, verify, then decide

Four fixable friction points (not one physical wall) can plausibly clear the target for a third of the budget. The unused capital is held in reserve, not spent pre-emptively on a plant the diagnosis doesn't support.

Why this isn't just an efficiency question: lead-time misses are already costing roughly $40M a year in lost and declined orders, and two framework customers (~18% of revenue) have tied contract renewal to fixing on-time delivery — against a $14.5–24M ask, this is a decision about retaining booked revenue, not just an operations tune-up. And the market backs the target: demand at a competitive lead time is estimated at ~52,000 units — 1,300 above the 50,700-unit target — so this fix is chasing real, growing demand (intake +9% YoY, backlog nearly doubled to 11 weeks), not manufacturing capacity nobody wants.

02 — Diagnosis

There is no single bottleneck. There's a chain of four fixable friction points.

Before the evidence: four terms come up throughout this brief. They're defined once, here, so nothing downstream assumes prior knowledge.

OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness)
Availability × Performance × Quality — a single score for how much of a machine's theoretical output is actually captured. 100% is perfect; most real plants run 40–85%. It's a diagnostic ratio, not a multiplier — OEE% × nameplate won't reproduce a plant's produced-units figure, since nameplate already reflects today's changeover and downtime losses.
OTIF (On-Time-In-Full)
The share of orders delivered complete, by the promised date. "Effective throughput" in this brief means units shipped OTIF — not just units produced.
WIP (Work-in-Process)
Units that have entered production but haven't shipped yet — inventory sitting mid-process. High WIP in front of a stage is a sign that stage can't keep up.
Constraint (Theory of Constraints)
The one stage in a chain that limits how much the whole system can produce. Speeding up any other stage doesn't add throughput — it just piles up work in front of the real constraint.
1
The front office is 95% queue, not work — a policy problem, not a capacity wall.
7.3 of the 14.2-week lead time (51%) elapses before a part is even cut. Actual hands-on-work across order entry, engineering, and scheduling totals ~16 hours — the other ~292 hours are queueing, caused by a shared 11-engineer pool with no triage, a weekly batch-release cutoff, and 6+ handoffs with no order owner. This is cheap and fast to fix — and it's why it's Lever 1.
2
Plant 1 is genuinely near-full — but a 29-point OEE gap at its bottleneck is real, uncaptured throughput.
90% utilized, 23,400 of 26,000 nameplate. Little raw slack — but the CNC bottleneck cell runs at only 71% OEE, losing output to changeovers (95 min × 6/day) and 8% downtime before any new equipment is needed.
3
Plant 2's "95% utilized" is the case's central trap — it's the friction, not the footprint.
Worst OEE (64%), worst downtime (15%), longest changeovers (140 min), worst quality (2.4% — already over the 1.8% ceiling on its own), worst unit cost (108). Expanding this plant, as Camp A proposed, would scale up the network's least-capable, highest-risk asset. Fixing it costs a fraction of expanding it.
4
Plant 3's idle capacity is real, but the constraint is people and mix, not machines.
58% utilized, 7,200 nameplate units sitting idle behind a 22% skilled-labor vacancy rate — and configured mostly for custom work, not the standard-product backlog that's actually backed up. The lever here is staffing and mix conversion, not new floor space.
Sequencing risk to manage, not ignore: speeding up the front office without also fixing Plant 2 doesn't add throughput — it just relocates the queue onto Plant 2's bottleneck. This is why the roadmap in §06 runs the Plant 2 fix in parallel with the front-office rollout, not after it.

06 — 30-month roadmap

08 — Risks & month-6 leading indicators

What could break this, and how the board will know by month 6.

Risks & dependencies

  • Plant 3 is the plan's real point of failure, not a footnote. It supplies over half the gross unit lift (+3,290 of the total). If it alone misses its ramp, effective throughput falls to ~48,316 — a 4.7% miss on target, not just an erased buffer. Labor-market and mix-conversion feasibility matter more than the topline buffer suggests.
  • Plant 2's nameplate "stretch" assumption (§data-model) is unverified — validate with a time-motion study in Wave 0 before committing Wave 2 capital, and gate its changeover-reduction work with an explicit quality-hold check, since Plant 2 already breaches the 1.8% ceiling alone.
  • Speeding the front office ahead of Plant 2 risks relocating WIP onto Plant 2's bottleneck instead of adding throughput — this is why Plant 2's fix runs alongside the front office (Wave 2, not Wave 4).
  • Engineering triage may face internal resistance — it changes how a shared, informally-run pool of 11 engineers is managed.

Month-6 KRIs the board should ask for

  • OTIF rate trend (target: visible movement off 81% toward 94%).
  • Plant 1 CNC OEE trajectory (target: moving off 71% toward ~82%).
  • Plant 2 changeover time & downtime % trend (target: 140min/15% both falling).
  • Plant 3 skilled-labor vacancy rate (target: moving off 22% toward ~10%).
  • Plant 2 WIP level (should not be rising — an early sign of the sequencing risk above).

10 — Decision requested today

Approve the phased spend. Not $48M, not zero.

Everything above is analysis. This is the ask.

The bottom line: Verona's plants aren't out of room — they're losing time to slow order handling, long machine changeovers, and one plant that's short-staffed. Fixing those four things gets us the growth target for about a third of the budget, and we'll know within six months whether it's working. We recommend funding the first step today.
Approve $14.5–24M of phased spend, with Wave 0 starting now. Wave 0 (diagnose & quick wins, M0–3) is the cheapest, lowest-risk step and the one that de-risks everything after it — it costs the least and confirms the two assumptions worth testing before Wave 2 capital commits (Plant 2's nameplate stretch, Plant 1's true bottleneck). The $24–33.5M reserve stays uncommitted today; you are not approving a fourth plant, and you're not being asked to rule one out either — you're asked to revisit that question at the month-6 checkpoint (§08) with real OTIF, OEE, and vacancy-rate data in hand, instead of a forecast.

Approve now
